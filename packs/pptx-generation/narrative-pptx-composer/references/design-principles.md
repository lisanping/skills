# Design Principles

Guiding principles, designer persona, and designer manifesto.
Consulted once at session start and whenever design trade-offs
arise. Absolute rules (banned typography / colors / layouts /
shapes) live in [design-guardrails.md](design-guardrails.md).

---

## Guiding Principles

Three pillars, in priority order. When they conflict, the
higher-priority pillar wins. **The priority order governs
conflicts, not minimums** — design never gets to be absent on
the grounds that content already won.

### 1 — Content: strategy before slides

- **Content first, design second.** Every presentation starts with
  *what to communicate* and *why*. Visual decisions are downstream of
  content decisions — never the reverse.
- **Every slide has a "so what."** If you can't write a
  `headlineMessage` for a slide, it doesn't deserve to exist.
  Cut or merge it.
- **The headline sequence tells the whole story.** Reading just
  the `headlineMessage` of every slide in order should produce a
  coherent argument. If it doesn't, the narrative is broken.
- **Data serves the argument, not the other way around.** Don't
  include data because it's available. Include it because it
  proves the `headlineMessage`. Every chart must answer "so what?"
- **Never fabricate.** All facts, figures, and sources must be
  traceable to user-provided content documents or the user's own
  brief. Never invent numbers, statistics, percentages, quotes,
  or attribution. When source material is insufficient, surface
  the gap to the user — do not fill it with plausible-sounding
  fiction. Generated content (cover, closing, dividers, summaries)
  must synthesize from existing material, not introduce new claims.
- **Terminology is a trust signal.** Inconsistent terms ("Digital
  Transformation" on one slide, "DX" on the next) signal sloppy
  thinking. The `s04a-terminology-registry.json` prevents this.

### 2 — Narrative: story-driven structure

- **Audience awareness drives every decision.** Who they are, what
  they know, and what power they have to act shapes purpose,
  density, formality, and storytelling pattern.
- **Transitions are explicit, not assumed.** The audience doesn't
  automatically see the connection between slides. Each transition
  must be designed — `transitionOut` / `transitionIn` make this a
  first-class design decision.
- **Information density follows emotional arc.** Light → dense →
  light matches audience attention. Front-loading dense data
  before establishing context loses the audience.
- **The closing must be actionable.** "Thank you" is not a
  closing. The last slide should state the specific ask or the
  single most important takeaway.

### 3 — Design: visual choices serve the narrative

Every slide carries two obligations: a content obligation (the
"so what") and an **aesthetic obligation** (a deliberate visual
move). Enforced via `aestheticMove` per slide (Step 5d) and
design-minimum quotas (layout variety, hero cadence, color-area,
typographic punch).

- **Every slide makes one deliberate aesthetic move.** Asymmetry,
  typographic weight contrast, color tension, whitespace rhythm,
  scale jump, geometric block, leader-line, or composition
  inversion — pick one and execute it well. "Title + bullets,
  no other moves" is a failure state for any slide, in any deck.
- **Restraint is a register, not a retreat.** An `authoritative`
  deck's aesthetic move may be a single confident asymmetry on a
  near-empty page; a `conversational` deck's may be a saturated
  color block. Restraint executed deliberately is high design.
  Sameness executed by default is not.
- **Designed from first principles, not templated.** Every layout
  and every decoration is generated by the LLM for this specific
  slide's content, audience, and narrative role — never selected
  from a fixed catalog. Spatial precision comes from explicit zone
  coordinates, not from a pre-computed pattern library.
- **Guardrails are absolute.** Design guardrails (banned fonts,
  banned color patterns, banned layout anti-patterns) override
  all aesthetic judgment. The full catalog lives in
  [design-guardrails.md](design-guardrails.md).
- **Restraint means abstract imagery, not no imagery.** On a
  technical / analytical / general deck, a bare typographic cover
  is a missed opportunity, not restraint.
- **Section slides are design moments.** Invest in the section
  title: oversized typographic treatment, asymmetric layout,
  accent color on a key word, full-bleed atmospheric background,
  or a single supporting visual motif. They should feel distinct
  from content pages.

## Designer Persona

In Step 5 and Step 8, Claude operates as a **senior presentation
designer** — not a content strategist (that's Steps 2–4) and not a
code generator (that's the execution layer). This persona:

- **Reasons explicitly before deciding.** For each slide: why this
  layout, why this density, why this contrast level, why these
  decorations.
- **Cannot see the output.** All spatial decisions go through
  predefined pattern coordinates, not visual intuition. The VLM
  subagent in Step 8 is the designer's eyes.

## Designer Manifesto

The deck's visual rhythm should feel composed, not assembled.
Body slides share a consistent spatial grammar while structural
slides (cover, dividers, closing) form a visual family. Variation
is intentional contrast, never drift.

**The two failure modes are equally bad.** Over-decoration —
ornament without intent — is the obvious failure. The less
obvious failure is *aesthetic absence* — the "no other moves"
failure state named in Pillar 3. Avoid both by demanding from
every slide: one deliberate `aestheticMove`, executed in service
of the message.
